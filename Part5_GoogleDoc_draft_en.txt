# Part 5: The AI Housing Scientist

A proof of concept that transfers the core ideas of AI Scientist-v2 to the domain of Japanese rental housing research

---

## 1. Overview

In this section, rather than using Sakana AI's AI Scientist-v2 as-is to generate a paper, I transferred its core idea, "trying multiple directions, evaluating the results, and selecting the most promising direction," to a different domain.

What I built is **The AI Housing Scientist**, an agent that helps users search for rental housing in Japan. It organizes the user's preferences, creates a research plan, gathers candidates through multiple search strategies, compares and evaluates the results, and then assists with drafting inquiry messages and checking contractual risks before signing.

One reason I chose this topic is that **searching for housing in Japan is itself an overly complex and often confusing problem**. Property information is scattered across multiple websites, and listing formats and field coverage are far from consistent. In addition, expired listings and conflicting conditions are common, while contractual issues such as initial fees, renewal fees, early termination penalties, and guarantor-company requirements are often difficult to understand in advance. For that reason, I believe that Japanese rental search requires more than simply entering conditions into a search box; it requires a system that helps users organize information, compare options, and identify what still needs to be checked.

I chose this topic because rental housing search is highly compatible with an AI Scientist-style design. There are three main reasons. First, property information is distributed across multiple websites, and each source has its own biases and missing fields. Second, there are strong trade-offs among rent, walking distance to the station, floor plan, and amenities. Third, the final output is not just information, but a practical decision: which property to contact and what to verify before signing. For this reason, the problem is better suited to trying multiple search strategies and selecting among them than to a single-shot search.

---

## 2. Understanding AI Scientist-v2 and How I Adapted It

Reading the AI Scientist-v2 code makes it clear that its core is not simply "paper generation." In practice, its key structure is that **an experiment manager controls the search stages, parallel agents create multiple candidate ideas, execution results are analyzed, and promising ideas are carried forward to the next stage**.

The part that mattered most to me was the following workflow. First, the overall search is divided into stages such as initial implementation, baseline improvement, creative improvement, and ablation. In each stage, it does not merely create several initial ideas; failed ideas are debugged, successful ideas are improved, and the tree continues to grow. Then, the execution results of each node, the extracted metrics, the generated plots, and the evaluation of those plots are all combined to decide which idea should be kept next. In addition, both successful and failed cases are accumulated in a journal and later converted into a report.

From this, I adopted **the idea of treating exploration as a tree, deciding the next branch based on observed results, and recording both successes and failures before summarizing them at the end**. At the same time, I did not directly reuse the parts that are specific to scientific experiments.

### 2.1 Core Functions I Adapted

To make this clearer, the elements I adapted from AI Scientist-v2 can be organized one by one as follows.

1. **Stage management**  
In AI Scientist-v2, there is a management layer that advances implementation, improvement, and analysis in stages.  
In my system, I mapped this to the rental-search flow of "clarifying conditions -> collecting candidates -> enriching detail pages -> integrity checking -> comparison -> inquiry preparation."

2. **Simultaneous expansion of multiple branches**  
In AI Scientist-v2, multiple initial ideas, improved ideas, and debugged ideas are explored in parallel.  
In my system, this became trying multiple search strategies at the same time, such as strict search, relaxed search, nearby-area expansion, source diversification, and detail-first search.

3. **Post-execution evaluation**  
In AI Scientist-v2, execution logs and metrics are analyzed to judge which idea is promising.  
In my system, each branch is evaluated using the number of search results, detail-page coverage, completeness of the fields needed for comparison, the quality of condition matching, source diversity, and the drop rate after integrity review.

4. **Selection of the best path**  
In AI Scientist-v2, the next surviving idea is chosen using multiple pieces of evidence rather than a single numeric score.  
Likewise, in my system, I do not simply favor the branch with the largest number of candidates. I prioritize branches that preserve the user's conditions and still leave rankable candidates after integrity review.

5. **Recording success and failure**  
In AI Scientist-v2, both successful and failed cases are stored in a journal and used for subsequent improvement.  
In my system as well, I record which search strategies worked and which failure patterns repeated, and I use them for final summarization and retry planning.

6. **Final summarization**  
In AI Scientist-v2, the exploration history is converted into a report.  
In my system, the selected search strategy, alternative strategies, failure patterns, recommended properties, and inquiry preparation are summarized and returned to the user.

An important point here is that **AI Scientist-v2 itself is not a simple highest-score selector**. In the code, the best node is chosen by combining not only numeric metrics but also execution-result analysis and plot evaluation. My implementation follows the same spirit: instead of selecting "the branch that found the most listings," it emphasizes the quality of condition matching, the richness of detail information, source reliability, and whether genuinely comparable candidates remain after integrity checking.

### 2.2 How It Differs from AI Scientist-v2

The differences between AI Scientist-v2 and my implementation are also clear. In AI Scientist-v2, each node is literally a piece of Python code, and the system automatically executes that code, detects bugs, extracts metrics, generates plots, and even evaluates those plots visually. It also includes baseline hyperparameter tuning, creative improvement, ablation studies, multi-seed evaluation, and aggregation of result plots.

In contrast, in my implementation, a node is not a "code proposal" but a **search strategy**. Accordingly, the evaluation target is not a learning curve or generated image, but the practical usefulness of the property candidates. Concretely, I check whether enough candidates remain, whether the necessary fields are available, whether off-area or expired listings are mixed in, and whether there is enough information to proceed to inquiry.

In other words, what I reused from AI Scientist-v2 was **the mechanism of exploring multiple alternatives, narrowing them based on observed results, and reusing the history for later decisions**, not the entire scientific code-generation pipeline. This was intentional, because in rental search the essential operations are condition relaxation, switching information sources, following detail pages, and checking consistency, rather than hyperparameter tuning or ablation.

## 3. What I Built

The AI Housing Scientist operates as follows.

1. The user enters their requirements in natural language.
2. The system structures those conditions and asks follow-up questions when needed.
3. It presents a research plan and begins the investigation after the user approves it.
4. It performs candidate collection, detail retrieval, and information organization through multiple search strategies.
5. It evaluates the collected candidates and selects the most promising candidate set.
6. It proceeds to candidate comparison, inquiry-message drafting, and pre-contract risk checking.

For example, if a user inputs, "I want to find a 1LDK near Shinjuku Station for under 100,000 yen, preferably pet-friendly," the system first extracts the budget, area, floor plan, and desired conditions. If necessary, it then confirms the move-in timing and acceptable walking distance to the station. After that, it runs several strategies in parallel, such as a search that strictly preserves the conditions, a search that relaxes them slightly to secure enough candidates, a search that expands to nearby areas, and a search that varies the query wording to find more sources. It then narrows the results to a set of candidates that can be compared clearly.

To describe more concretely what this implementation actually does, consider a vague request such as, "I want to find an apartment for living alone in Kasukabe." The system first asks follow-up questions and fills in missing information such as budget, floor plan, walking distance to the station, and move-in timing. Based on those answers, it generates multiple search queries. The important point here is that it does not perform only a search that perfectly matches the conditions. It also performs **slightly relaxed searches in parallel** so that the result does not collapse to zero matches.

The web research also does more than simply return a single search-results page. It first performs a relatively simple query. If the returned page is a listing page, it then follows candidate property detail pages linked from that listing. LLMs are also used to decide which detail pages should be prioritized, so this is not just URL collection. The system actively drills down into pages that are meaningful as actual property candidates.

## 4. Technical Contributions

### 4.1 Treating Housing Search as a Search Problem

The central design decision in this implementation is to treat housing search not as a simple lookup but as a **search problem**.

Ordinary real-estate search often runs a single condition set once and stops there. In reality, however, problems frequently occur: if the conditions are enforced strictly, there may be too few candidates; if they are relaxed, the candidate pool grows but quality declines; and if the wording is changed, different information sources may appear. Therefore, in my implementation, multiple search strategies are assumed from the start, and the system can switch among them depending on what is happening.

Concretely, the strategies include the following.

- Search strictly under the original conditions to find the primary candidates.
- Relax the conditions slightly to recover candidate count.
- Expand the search to nearby areas.
- Change search wording to reduce source bias.
- Prioritize candidates that are more likely to yield rich detail information.
- Further exploit the neighborhood of conditions that already looked promising.

This not only helps recover candidates that would be missed by a single-shot search, but also makes it easier to explain why a particular candidate set was selected.

### 4.2 Evaluating and Selecting Search Results

The most important AI Scientist-style element is not simply listing multiple search results, but introducing a mechanism to **evaluate and choose** among them.

In this implementation, each search strategy is evaluated at least from the following perspectives.

- How many candidates remain.
- How much information could be enriched from detail pages.
- How complete the key comparison fields are, such as rent, station distance, and floor plan.
- How close the top candidates are to the user's requirements.
- Whether the information sources are overly concentrated.
- Whether there are too many duplicates or too many candidates dropped by integrity review.

Moreover, the final selection is not simple score maximization. In housing search, a result with "many candidates but badly degraded conditions" is less useful than a result with "fewer candidates but strong condition match and complete comparison information." For that reason, my evaluation design emphasizes not only candidate count but also strictness of condition match and quality of information.

### 4.3 Real-Estate-Specific Integrity Checking

In rental-property search, it is easy for expired listings, pricing inconsistencies, for-sale properties, or out-of-area properties to mix into the results. Therefore, in my implementation, I placed an independent **integrity-checking layer** before ranking.

This layer mainly checks the following points.

- Whether the listing appears outdated.
- Whether numeric values such as rent are inconsistent.
- Whether for-sale listings are mixed into a rental search.
- Whether the property location is substantially outside the requested area.
- Whether the floor plan or required conditions are clearly mismatched.

By adding this layer, the design moves away from "keep it because it appeared in search results" and toward "keep only candidates that are trustworthy enough to compare." This is an important difference from a generic property-search service.

### 4.4 Extending from Research to Action

This implementation is not a system that simply returns a list of candidates and stops. In addition to candidate comparison, it integrates **inquiry-message drafting** and **pre-contract risk checking** into the same workflow.

At minimum, the contract-risk module extracts the following kinds of items.

- Renewal fees
- Early termination penalties
- Notice periods for cancellation
- Conditions related to guarantor companies

As a result, the system does not merely search for information. It supports the next step the user should take.

## 5. Difference from Deep Research

This implementation is also different from a so-called **Deep Research** agent.

Deep Research-style systems are strong at broadly investigating multiple information sources, organizing the results, and returning a relatively long research report. In contrast, the primary goal of my implementation is not to investigate broadly and stop there, but to **try multiple search strategies themselves, evaluate the outcomes, and narrow them down into concrete next-action candidates**.

The difference can be summarized as follows.

- Deep Research is centered on "research and summarization."
- The AI Housing Scientist is centered on "trying multiple ways of searching and selecting the best one."
- Deep Research is strong at explaining information.
- The AI Housing Scientist puts more weight on decision support, such as candidate exclusion, candidate adoption, and inquiry preparation.
- Deep Research often ends with a research report.
- The AI Housing Scientist continues through inquiry drafting and contract-risk checking.

Therefore, this project is more accurately understood not as "a real-estate version of Deep Research," but as **a system that layers AI Scientist-style exploration and selection on top of Deep Research-style information gathering**.

---

## 6. What Is Novel

The novelty of this implementation is not that I built an interface that can search for properties using natural language. The core novelty lies in the following three points.

- It treats housing search not as a single-shot query, but as exploration through multiple strategies.
- It evaluates those search results, prunes weak directions, and retains strong ones.
- It integrates comparison, inquiry preparation, and contract checking into a single workflow.

In other words, I did not build a generic real-estate chatbot. The essence of this project is that **I transplanted the AI Scientist-v2 structure of exploration, evaluation, and selection into a real-world applied domain**.

## 7. Technical Stack

The implementation uses React and TypeScript for the frontend, and FastAPI and Python 3.11 for the backend. Candidate collection combines a search API with a mock property database, and SQLite is used for storing history and audit information.

In addition, I implemented the system in separate components for condition clarification, search management, candidate evaluation, integrity checking, inquiry generation, and contract-risk checking. The backend also includes more than 100 test cases. Although this is a proof of concept, it is not just a demo; the major logic is structured so that it can be validated reproducibly.

This task is not in the same category as extremely difficult problems such as mathematical theorem proving or advanced program synthesis. It is mainly about natural-language condition clarification, search-strategy generation, page summarization, and information extraction. For that reason, I made multiple practical models available and compared them. Specifically, I tested `GPT-OSS-120B`, `GPT-5.4-mini`, `Gemini 2.5 Flash`, `Claude Sonnet 4.6`, and `Qwen3-32B`. `GPT-OSS-120B` and `Qwen3-32B` were used via Groq.

Based on my observations, **the model with the best balance of execution speed, cost, and output quality was GPT-OSS-120B on Groq**. By contrast, smaller models sometimes failed to follow the required output format consistently, were overly influenced by few-shot examples and ended up searching for properties in irrelevant regions, or failed to extract prices and property images correctly. In particular, with smaller models, instability in information extraction was more likely to degrade the overall search quality.

This is not a rigorous benchmark, but an observation obtained during implementation and trial runs. Still, at least in this system, I observed the tendency that **stronger models achieved higher success rates in planning, structured output, and information extraction**. This is consistent with the broader AI Scientist intuition that stronger foundation models are more likely to produce higher-quality outcomes.

For reference, using the official public API prices available as of April 14, 2026, the approximate cost of processing one million input tokens and one million output tokens is as follows. Gemini uses standard pricing, and Groq uses ordinary token-based pricing.

| Model | Input Price | Output Price | Total |

This comparison also shows that GPT-OSS-120B on Groq was low-cost while still providing sufficiently high performance, giving it the best price-performance balance in this project. By contrast, Claude Sonnet 4.6 offers high quality but is the most expensive option and is therefore too expensive for routine use. Gemini 2.5 Flash sits in the middle and is a reasonable choice when one wants a balance of speed and quality.

## 8. Limitations

Of course, the current system has clear limitations.

- Integration with real data sources is still limited, and the system depends partly on mock data and search results.
- The evaluation of search results is based on heuristics, not on a learned domain-specific evaluator.
- Contract-risk checking is only a first-pass check and is not a substitute for legal advice.
- There is not yet a mechanism that automatically improves the search strategies themselves.

Therefore, the most accurate way to position this system is not as a finished product, but as a **proof of concept for testing whether an AI Scientist-style design can also be effective in a real-world domain such as rental housing search**.

---

## 9. Future Directions

The following three directions seem particularly promising for future development.

1. Automatically improving search strategies and evaluation weights using evolutionary methods
2. Building datasets about listing freshness, factual consistency, and inquiry usefulness, and training domain-specific evaluators
3. Connecting the system to real operational data and real APIs, and quantitatively comparing the effectiveness of exploration and selection

---

## 10. Conclusion

What I wanted to show in this section is that the value of AI Scientist-v2 lies not only in machine-learning paper generation itself, but in its reusable design pattern of **trying multiple directions, evaluating the results, and selecting the most promising direction**.

The AI Housing Scientist is a proof of concept that transfers that design into the domain of Japanese rental housing search. For a real-world domain where information is incomplete and comparison and decision-making are essential, it demonstrates, as a concrete system, that an AI Scientist-style loop of exploration and selection can be effective.
